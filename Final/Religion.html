The Hittites were an ancient Anatolian Indo-European civilization that flourished in the Bronze Age, centered in what is now modern-day Turkey and Syria. They established their kingdom around 1800 BC, with their capital at Hattusa (modern-day Boğazkale) in north-central Anatolia.

At its peak, the Hittite Empire was a major power in the ancient Near East, controlling a vast territory that stretched across most of modern Turkey, Syria, and parts of the northern Levant. They had complex relationships with other major powers of the time, such as the New Kingdom of Egypt and the Assyrian Empire in Mesopotamia.

The Hittites are renowned for their advanced legal system, as evidenced by the Hittite laws, one of the oldest surviving written legal codes. They were also skilled in architecture and art, with impressive fortified cities, rock-cut reliefs, and sculptures.

In terms of religion, the Hittites worshipped a pantheon of deities, including the storm god Teshub and the sun goddess Arinna. Their language, known as Hittite, was an Indo-European language written in cuneiform script.

The Hittite Empire reached its peak under the reign of Suppiluliuma I (1344–1322 BC), who expanded the empire's territory through military conquests. He defeated the Mitanni kingdom, capturing their capital Washukanni and annexing their Syrian vassals. His son Piyassili was installed as the viceroy of Carchemish, solidifying Hittite control over Syria.

However, after 1180 BC, the Hittite Empire began to disintegrate, fragmenting into several independent "Neo-Hittite" city-states that survived until the 8th century BC. The reasons for the empire's decline are not entirely clear, but it is believed that a combination of internal instability, external invasions, and environmental factors played a role.

Despite its eventual decline, the Hittites left a lasting legacy in the ancient Near East, influencing the cultures and civilizations that followed in the region.
